Description

The possible existence of life beyond Earth is one of the most fundamental questions relating the human race to the physical Universe around us. While considerations of extraterrestrial life were in the domains of philosophy and science fiction in the past, today we have considerable scientific knowledge that can be applied to the question. Advances in modern astronomy have dramatically improved our understanding of our cosmic surroundings: tracing the cosmic origins of biological molecules, direct evidence for planets around most stars (exoplanets), and a deeper understanding of planet formation have all emerged in the past generation. Several fields -- geology, biochemistry, paleontology (fossils), evolutionary biology -- give insight (though not a complete understanding) on how life arose on Earth, and spacecraft travel provides constraints on life elsewhere in our Solar System.

The results from studies on Earth, the Solar System, and exoplanets across the Galaxy together indicate that the conditions for life are widespread in the Universe; extraterrestrial life has not been found yet, but does not seem implausible. Interstellar space travel between habitable planets, by humans or other beings, is exceedingly difficult but not physically impossible. The course is thus rooted in modern science, but is connected to the long-standing philosophical question: Are we alone in the Universe? Today, this question is often embedded in science fiction (such as movies about aliens) and discussion about whether aliens have visited Earth. Today, astronomical studies are leading the way in answering part of the question: Earth-like planets seems to be extremely common. But there is no evidence yet for life outside of Earth.

Activities

Part of the course resembles a traditional classroom learning environment: We have lectures by the instructor (online rather than in person), read chapters of our textbook, and take quizzes to evaluate that we have learned the material. But the course also has more (inter)active assignments: students participate in discussion forums; take part in a huge citizen science research effort; and engage in science writing, producing two papers. The result is a multifaceted learning experience, blending more passive knowledge acquisition with more active learning processes.

Grading

Quizzes (48%)

Four times during the semester after a Theme is completed, students will take a quiz on recent material covered in the class, associated reading and online material. The quizzes will last 40 minutes and have combinations of multiple choice, short answer and short essay questions. Students may have access to notes and the textbook, but study prior to the quiz is necessary because of the time constraint. Quizzes are taken in Penn State's ANGEL Web environment. Students can take the quiz anytime during specified 4-day periods from early-Friday to end-of-day Monday. Quizzes are `open book, open Web' format without proctoring; however, they are timed so that students are who are not familiar with the material cannot perform well. Each Quiz is worth 12% of the course grade.

Science Writing Papers (27%)

Students will submit two original papers using topics and resources provided by (or in agreement with) the instructor. The goal is to learn techniques of science writing: the papers review scientific findings and will present them in a formal style. General guidence is given here, and the student and instructor interact individually on Paper #1. The student then writes Paper #2 on their own; it is the same length as Paper #1 but is worth more points as the student can incorporate what they have leared about science writing. There are three deadlines: a draft of Paper 1, the final version of Paper 1 and, at the beginning of Exam Week, delivery of Paper 2. Details on science writing, paper requirements, and sample topics are given here. Paper #1 is worth 12% of the course grade, and Paper #2 is worth 15% of the course grade.

Discussion Forums (15%)

Throughout the course, we will hold informal discussions on through PSU's ANGEL, and optionally VoiceThread, environment. The instructor will start with a question, sometimes accompanied with on-line material. Students then participate with independent or secondary commentaries. Points are earned by number and quality of contributions; contributions based on factual material is preferred over informal opinions. The maximum contribution is 15% of the course grade.

PlanetHunters Activity (10%)

During Weeks 8-9, students will engage in the Zooniverse `citizen science' activity PlanetHunters. This involves a training session, classification of ~30 lightcurves from NASA's Kepler mission for possible planet transits, and completion of a worksheet and analysis. The goal is to examine observations from the Kepler observations of ~200,000 stars, characterizing the variability of the star and looking for periodic dips in brightness due to transiting planets.

At the end of the semester, course grades (A/A-/B+/B/B-/C+/C/D/F) will be calculated on a `curve' based on the instructor's judgment of class performance. Throughout the semester, each student can access their grades for past work on the ANGEL gradebook.
