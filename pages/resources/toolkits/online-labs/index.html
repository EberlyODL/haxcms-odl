With all this talk of contingency planning, many of our colleagues are asking "How do you deal with labs in the face of a cancellation?" And the answer, as always, is "It depends." 

If we are talking about a University shutdown of a couple of weeks, think about how you could flexibly work around the concepts and procedures that need to be taught and the timing. Is this something that could be pushed back? Could you move another lab up that might translate better to online? Is there a video out there that shows somebody doing the lab? (There is an extensive video database at the Libraries.) Is there a report or case study that a student could read and then write up a report on how they might proceed or what they might do? Could they read a chapter from a book, like Napolean's Buttons and apply concepts to modern innovations? Is there a dataset they could analyze? I recently saw a post from one of our Bioinformatics courses that is actually having students perform work on COVID-19 analysis! How amazing is that? Below are numerous resources to animations, simulations, and other content that might be relevant. Again, think about this as an opportunity to approach labs from a different perspective, temporarily. At some point, we will all go back to normal, but why not think about something that still gets students thinking like a scientists and applying higher order skills to a novel situation?

In February 2020, Melissa Hicks was asked to present as part of a two-week virtual conference. She presented on the "Online Lab Conundrum," which was based on the question, what would you do if your dean asked you to create an online lab class for next semester?   • CitizenScience.gov: an official government website designed to accelerate the use of crowdsourcing and citizen science across the U.S. government. In citizen science, the public participates voluntarily in the scientific process, addressing real-world problems
  • CitizenScience.org: Citizen science is the involvement of the public in scientific research – whether community-driven research or global investigations. The Citizen Science Association unites expertise from educators, scientists, data managers, and others to power citizen science.
  • SciStarter.org: SciStarter is an online community dedicated to improving the citizen science experience for project managers and participants.  Over 3,000 projects and events are searchable by location, scientific topic, and age level, and by joining SciStarter, members can track their contributions and provide valuable feedback.  SciStarter also supports researchers in managing projects, including best practices for engaging participant partners.

    • Darwin's Ark: The Darwin’s Ark project team needs pet survey information, DNA samples and, for a tick disease project, actual ticks you collect. Your data will benefit pets and their humans, too!
