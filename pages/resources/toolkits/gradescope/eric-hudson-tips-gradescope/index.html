• Be sure students have access to a device with a camera. I've used Gradescope for two semesters and I have never had a student that didn't have some way of taking a picture of their work. Quality of the image can range widely, but there are a number of free apps online and through Adobe (free to Penn State students) that can improve quality. Ask students with the best submissions which apps they're using, how they're using them, and then share that info with your next students.
  • Every assignment I do in the class, I use Gradescope, so homework, quizzes, even the midterm. At the end of the midterm, they just walk up to the front of the room. Scan their exam, upload it into to Gradescope and then they can walk away with their original exam.
  • Use the templates feature to create consistent assignment experiences.
  • Gradescope will automatically group the responses that students make that look the same. When it's time to grade, rather than having to grade every single assignment, I just grade the groups. If 22 students have the same response, you just graded 22 students with a single click.
  • Everything is based on an additive or subtracted rubric that can be modified as you go. So you can either have your points add up, or if students do make certain mistakes, then it's subtracts points off. The rubric also suports LaTex.
  • It's very easy for multiple people to be grading at the same time. Gradescope will just skip over assignments if someone else has a document open; it won't let you open it twice at the same time.
  • Gradescope is great for group work. I do group quizzes, for example, and so when the students submit their work, they just tag everyone in the class who was responsible for that assignment and then everyone gets that grade.
