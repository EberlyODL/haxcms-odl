If we are talking about a University shutdown of a couple of weeks, think about how you could flexibly work around the concepts and procedures that need to be taught and the timing. Is this something that could be pushed back? Could you move another lab up that might translate better to online? Is there a video out there that shows somebody doing the lab? (There is an extensive video database at the Libraries.) Is there a report or case study that a student could read and then write up a report on how they might proceed or what they might do? Could they read a chapter from a book, like Napolean's Buttons and apply concepts to modern innovations? Is there a dataset they could analyze?

I recently saw a post from one of our Bioinformatics courses that is actually having students perform work on COVID-19 analysis! How amazing is that? Below are numerous resources to animations, simulations, and other content that might be relevant. Again, think about this as an opportunity to approach labs from a different perspective, temporarily. At some point, we will all go back to normal, but why not think about something that still gets students thinking like a scientists and applying higher order skills to a novel situation?

Presentation: The Online Lab Conundrum

In February 2020, Melissa Hicks was asked to present as part of a two-week virtual conference. She presented on the "Online Lab Conundrum," which was based on the question, what would you do if your dean asked you to create an online lab class for next semester? Melissa shares some background on online labs, the theory that can help support them, and examples and tools to help you as you think about developing online labs.

How to Think about and Create Online Labs

Where do I start? When thinking about online labs, you need to start with the objectives. What do your students need to know, think, or feel? We hear often that faculty believe students need to be in a lab so they can burn themselves on a flame or perform a specific technical procedure. Do you have them perform this on the test? Are there other ways we can look at these objectives and get students to where they need to be.

Online labs are little ecosystems of learning that are comprised of several different parts. You need to think not only about the objectives, but strategies for engagement, academic integrity, tools you can use, accessibility, risk, and more.

Overwhelming, we know. But it can be done.

Steps for creating an online lab

Think about what can be done in the online space and then think about what MUST be done in person. Things like smelling the results or a reaction or feeling the heat of a bunsen burner are things one must experience in person. But when it comes to things like measurement or observation, many of these things, given the right choice of multimedia and activities can be done online.
