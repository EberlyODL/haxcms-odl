When you require synchronous learning, you are putting a burden on the student to structure their life around a time-based commitment. At the same time, however, you are also helping students who are not used to distance education to find structure and space to focus on what they need to learn.

Some faculty employ a mix of both. They record a lecture in advance (asynchronous) and ask students to come to class (synchronous) to perform some sort of active learning. Or they may ask student to attend a live lecture (synchronous) and post a recording of the event so students can access it later (asynchronous.)

Synchronous Learning

Synchronous learning is learning that occurs at a specific time via a specific medium. It can happen via video- or tele-conferencing, live chatting, or live-streaming lectures.

Advantages

  • Provides students and faculty a familiar context that maintains the pace of the course and the invaluable opportunity for faculty-student interaction.
  • Gives students much needed structure.
  • Preserves the sense of community and classroom culture.
  • Allows students to ask the instructor questions and receive live feedback
  • Useful for imparting key ideas or providing a forum to many.

Disadvantages

  • Students may be in different time zone.
  • Students may have to work around family schedules or computer use to be in class.
  • Students may have other obligations to attend to when they are situated at home or outside of class, such as a job or a child.

Asynchronous Learning

Advantages

  • Allows for students who are shy or introverts to have the ability to think in detail and respond how they want.
  • Allows for instructors to build more flexible and personalized interactions by utilizing ed tech tools.
  • May provide several different ways/modes for students to learn.
  • Could potentially be very simple, requiring low bandwidth and simple tools.
  • Struggling with questions or answers may provide deeper learning opportunities for students.
  • If students are working in groups, they can have some control over their own learning experience and time schedule.
  • Good for community knowledge-building.
  • Inherently self-reflective and more conducive to deep learning.

Disadvantages

  • Students may not know the best approaches and require scaffolding.
  • Students may feel disconnected.
  • Students do not get instant feedback or responses to question.
  • Students may not be motivated.
