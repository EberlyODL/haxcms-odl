This past weekend (February 1-3) the Office of Digital Learning (Eberly College of Science at Penn State) participated in the eighth consecutive Drupal Camp NJ , hosted at Princeton University. The annual event brings developers and industry professionals from varying backgrounds and skill-levels together to share all things Drupal through engaging presentations, co-working sessions, and networking.

This year there were a number of great discussions to get involved with, but one topic continued to dominate the conversation - decoupling Drupal. Decoupling Drupal essentially means separating Drupal’s front-end (theme layer) from it’s back-end (data/content layer) using a custom JavaScript framework. While there are still some discussions as to which framework is ‘best’ when decoupling Drupal, the general consensus is that the decoupled method is becoming an industry-wide movement.

Jesus Manuel Olivas (Head of Products at weKnow ) showcased decoupling tools like Gatsby and Tome . These tools assist in creating static sites by decoupling Drupal’s theme and just using Drupal for content creation.

Saurabh Chugh and Surendra Singh hosted a discussion, ‘Decoupling Patterns, Services and creating an enterprise level editorial experience’ where they also emphasized using decoupled applications to display content.

Our team demonstrated decoupled development when Michal Potter and Charles Lavera joined ELMS:LN lead developer, Bryan Ollendyke (College of Arts and Architecture - Penn State) to present work on web components. Specifically, the trio discussed the tooling they use to quickly generate and publish their components onto the web (WCFactory / Drupal webcomponents module ). In addition, Bryan explored the future of content-authoring with a completely headless authoring experience called HAX.
