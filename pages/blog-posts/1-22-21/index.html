With recent restrictions creating the demand for more engaging video content, our office set out to to create our own home-office / mobile lightboard. Making lightboards at home isn't something new. In fact, people have been creating their own lightboards for awhile and there are some really great resources out there to get you started.

For the purposes of this project, I wanted to see if I could build our team lightboard using one of those resources while adding additional documentation and comparing costs for materials. In this example, I use Steve Griffith's article, 'How to Make a Lightboard for less than $100'. The article was written in 2016, and is featured on Flip Learning's website. Can we still do this for less than $100 in 2021? Let's find out!

Planning

Before starting the project, I spent some time considering the overall size. I wanted something large enough to capture the talent and information being shared, but small enough it would easily fit inside most home-offices and vehicles for transportation; I decided on a 4' wide x 3' design.

Materials

  • 4 pine boards for the frame (I used 4' long, 1' 3/4" wide, 3/4" thick planks) It's important to pay attention to the overall thickness because we'll use a router to carve out a 1/2" channel for the recessed lights to sit in later.
  • 2 short pine 2x4's for the base (I used 3 1/2' long boards)
  • 3M ultra clear frame glass (cut to size)
  • LED Light Strip (I used 16ft and trimmed off the access on the LED trimline)
  • Wood Screws / Glue
  • 2 Shelf brackets
  • 6 coach screws, washers, nuts (to attach frame to base)
  • 6 short screws (to attach shelf brackets to the frame) Don't go too long in length on this screw or it will come through the otherside of the frame
  • 1 pack of Neon Dry-Erase Markers

Step 1

Making sure to be center, use a router or bench saw to cut a 1/2" wide x 1/2" deep recessed channel down the inside edge of each of the 4 pine frame boards.

Step 2

Mitre, mark, and cut the four corners to create the lightboard frame. You can also use this opportunity to drill a hole in one side of the frame so that the electical chord from the LED light strip can pass through; I kept mine towards the bottom.

Step 3

Using your two short pines (for the base), trim off 5" from the end of each board. Attach those trimmed blocks to the remaining board to create a support for your lightboard frame to rest against.

Step 4

Using the wood glue and wood screws, begin to assemble the frame. Also begin attaching the bracket shelves and frame to the base using the coach screws, washers, and nuts. Important Note: Leave one end of your frame unglued so you can remove it later and slide in the glass.

Step 5

Once you're certain everything is fitting together well, remove the frame from the base and paint; I used flat-black spray paint.

Step 6

Once everything is dry, it's time to insert your LED light strip. Carefully press your LED light strip into the recessed channel of your lightboard. If your LED light strip is too long, you will need to trim it to fit. Important Note: If you need to trim your LED light strip, be sure to trim ONLY on the LED strip trim-line or you may break the LED strip; refer to your specific light product's instructions.

Step 7

If you've made it this far, it's time to order the glass. I took the frame to my local custom-frame shop in Altoona, and they were able to measure and order a piece of 3M ultra clear glass to fit. It did take awhile, but once the glass came in, I brought it home and started the install.

Using caution (the edges / corners of the glass are sharp), remove the secton of the frame that you left unglued in Step 4. Once removed, slowly and CAREFULLY, slide your glass down the recessed channel and into place. The glass will rest directly on the LED light strip; that's okay.

Step 8

Once you have your glass installed and the frame is reattached, connect your lightboard to the base, tighten nuts, and plug it in for a test run.

Final Cost

With our lightboard finished, it's time to add up our cost to see if we were able to come in under the $100 mark.

  • 4 pine boards for the frame: $20
  • 2 short pine 2x4's for the base: $7
  • 3M ultra clear frame glass (cut to size): $40
  • LED Light Strip: $50
  • 2 Shelf brackets: $20
  • Screws / washers / nuts: $10
  • 1 pack of Neon Dry-Erase Markers: $13
  • Misc items I had around the house and didn't need to buy, but still cost money (paint, glue): $10
  • Total: $157

    • Conclusions

    Unfortunately, I came in $57 over budget. I attribute this mostly to the fact that I built a slightly larger lightboard than the one used in the example article, and I also think the cost of some of these items has increased since the article was originally written in 2016. However, even over budget, this was still an extremely cost-effective solution for creating more engaging video content.
